New Zealand Healthcare Is Under Pressure. Our Skills Can Still Make a Difference.

There is a particular kind of helplessness that comes from watching a health system strain.

Most of us are not health ministers. We do not control hospital budgets, workforce pipelines, GP enrolment capacity, specialist waitlists, or the architecture of national health data. We cannot magic up nurses, psychiatrists, rural doctors, aged-care beds, or timely appointments by caring harder.

But we can notice where people are falling through the cracks. And if we have a useful skill, we can turn that noticing into something practical.

That is the thought behind two small public-good projects I have been building: Care Finder Aotearoa, a low-barrier mental health support finder, and Code Red Aotearoa, an evidence-led website about pressure points in New Zealand healthcare and mental health access.

They are not grand solutions. They are not substitutes for properly funded services, safe staffing, strong primary care, or political accountability. They are small attempts to make the next step clearer for people who are already carrying too much.

And I think that distinction matters.

The pitfall is not just scarcity. It is navigation.

When we talk about the problems in New Zealand healthcare, we often talk about numbers: workforce shortages, wait times, under-resourced services, rural access, pressure on emergency departments, and the growing gap between what people need and what the system can reliably provide.

Those numbers are real. Health New Zealand's own workforce planning describes current medical workforce shortages, including an estimated 1,810 doctor FTE shortfall overall, a 210 FTE psychiatry shortage, and a 220 FTE GP shortage. It also notes that New Zealand can only train a limited number of doctors each year, with a medical school cap of 614 places as of the start of 2025. The Ministry of Health's Rural Health Strategy says plainly that rural communities' health needs are often under-served, especially when it comes to access.

But there is another pitfall sitting underneath the numbers: people do not experience "the health system" as a spreadsheet. They experience it as a maze.

They experience it as:

  • not knowing whether their problem is "bad enough"
  • not knowing who to call first
  • not knowing whether they will be eligible
  • not knowing what it will cost
  • not knowing how to describe what is happening
  • not knowing whether they will be believed
  • not knowing if a referral has disappeared into the void
  • not knowing what to do while they wait

For mental health in particular, that first step can be brutal. When someone is anxious, depressed, burnt out, grieving, isolated, ashamed, or in crisis-adjacent territory, "just find support" is not a simple instruction. It is an administrative task wrapped in fear.

This is one of the quieter failures of healthcare design: we often assume that the person seeking help has enough energy, confidence, literacy, money, and time to navigate the system. Many people do not.

We should not mistake tools for services.

I want to be careful here. Technology cannot care for a person in the way a clinician, counsellor, peer worker, nurse, GP, social worker, or trusted community member can.

A website cannot hold someone's hand in an emergency department. A search tool cannot replace a funded appointment. A static page cannot repair burnout in the workforce.

That is why Care Finder Aotearoa is deliberately framed as a navigation aid, not a clinical service. It is designed to help someone take a first step when they need mental health support but do not know who to contact or what to say. It includes a guided intake flow, provider search, funding guidance, and a simple first-contact message builder. It keeps answers in the browser rather than demanding an account.

The goal is modest: reduce friction.

Sometimes that is still meaningful. A person who was going to give up might send the email. A person who felt stupid for not knowing where to start might realise the system is simply hard to navigate. A person worried about cost might see funding pathways they had not heard of. A person who cannot find the words might use a draft message as a starting point.

Small does not mean trivial.

Public pressure also needs better evidence.

Code Red Aotearoa came from a related frustration: public debate about healthcare often becomes either too abstract or too partisan.

People know something is wrong. They see it in ambulance ramping, delayed specialist care, exhausted clinicians, stretched aged care, mental health bottlenecks, and the way private contracting can be presented as a simple fix to problems that are anything but simple.

But if we want public pressure to be useful, it needs to be legible. It needs receipts. It needs to help people move from anger to action.

That is why Code Red Aotearoa is built around pressure points: mental health access, nurse staffing, aged care, clinical workforce pressure, justice-system overflow, and private-contracting risks. The project includes a campaign toolkit with an evidence pack, MP email template, and local action alerts.

Again, this is not the solution. It is scaffolding for civic action.

Healthcare is political because budgets, priorities, laws, contracts, staffing models, and accountability structures are political. But it is also personal because the consequences land on bodies, families, clinicians, and communities. Good civic tools should help connect those two realities without flattening either one.

The most useful question is: what can I make smaller?

When a problem is enormous, it is tempting to aim only at enormous answers.

But most individual contributions start with a smaller question:

What can I make smaller?

If you are a software developer, maybe you can make a confusing pathway easier to search. Maybe you can turn scattered public data into a clearer view. Maybe you can build a tool that helps people write to an MP, compare local services, or understand eligibility.

If you are a designer, maybe you can make forms less hostile. Maybe you can improve plain-language explanations. Maybe you can help a community group present information in a way that people can actually use when they are stressed.

If you are a writer, maybe you can translate policy into human language. Maybe you can document what is happening locally. Maybe you can interview people who are affected and make sure their stories are not reduced to statistics.

If you are a clinician, maybe your skill is pattern recognition: naming the bottlenecks that outsiders cannot see. If you are a data person, maybe it is cleaning and visualising evidence. If you are a teacher, maybe it is helping people understand their rights. If you are a neighbour, maybe it is helping someone make a call, get to an appointment, or write down symptoms before seeing a GP.

Not every contribution needs to be digital. Not every contribution needs to scale. Sometimes the most important work is local, relational, and quiet.

But there is power in asking: where is the friction, and what skill do I have that could reduce it?

Open work invites better work.

One reason I put these projects on GitHub is that public-good work benefits from being inspectable.

If a tool helps people find care, people should be able to see what it does and challenge what it gets wrong. If a site makes claims about healthcare pressure points, those claims should be traceable. If a project uses public information, the way that information is handled should be open to scrutiny.

Open source is not automatically ethical. A public repository does not make a project accurate, safe, or useful. But openness creates the possibility of correction. It invites other people to bring their expertise: clinicians, advocates, researchers, lived-experience voices, policy people, accessibility testers, translators, and local communities.

That matters in healthcare because harm can hide in confident design. A tool that looks polished can still mislead people. A dataset can be out of date. A pathway can exclude the people who most need it. A neat interface can accidentally imply certainty where none exists.

So the work has to stay humble.

The right posture is not "I built an app, therefore I fixed healthcare." The right posture is "I saw a point of friction, I made something that might help, and I am open to improving it."

We need systems change. We also need citizen competence.

There is a false choice we should reject: either demand systemic change, or make small practical things.

We need both.

We need properly funded public services, sustainable workforce planning, genuine rural access, better mental health pathways, stronger aged care, and accountability that measures what patients and clinicians actually experience. We need to treat healthcare infrastructure as infrastructure, not as a series of heroic rescues performed by exhausted people.

But while we push for that, we can also become more competent citizens.

Competent citizens do not wait passively for perfect institutions. They learn how systems work. They share knowledge. They build tools. They document failures. They support people through the maze. They make public evidence easier to understand. They notice when language is being used to obscure reality. They ask better questions.

Most of all, they refuse the comfort of helplessness.

I am a software developer in Christchurch. My skill is not clinical care. It is building small, usable things out of messy information. So that is where I have started.

Care Finder Aotearoa tries to make the first step toward mental health support less intimidating.

Code Red Aotearoa tries to make the pressure points in healthcare more visible and actionable.

They are small. They are incomplete. They will need correction.

But they are something.

And in a strained health system, "something" can be the beginning of a larger habit: using what we know, where we are, for the people who are nearest to the cracks.
